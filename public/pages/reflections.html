English Composition I: Reflection

English 1311, Composition I had four major assignments, one of them being the completion of this reflection. The other three assignments were a series of essays completed throughout the semester, each equipped with various processes of writing which led to rough and polished drafts. Though some challenges persisted from the beginning to the end of the semester, clear growth can be observed throughout the processes of each assignment.

Projects and Their Processes

Project One

The first essay assignment was a "This I Believe" essay, based on the essay series of the same name. Tasked with constructing a narrative of a significant personally held belief, I dedicated this assignment to a particular philosophy that has come to fruition in the past few years. "I Live For Me" was an accurate title for the essay that compactly dictated my journey to living for myself rather than for the approval and acceptance of others. The essay emphasizes the realization that one's existence is not an indebted obligation but an opportunity for personal growth and self-expression. The analogy of slowly deteriorating painted ceiling tiles served as a metaphor for my perception of self as a mural shaped by my parents' assumed expectations. "I Live For Me" concluded with a newfound perspective and encapsulated the view of personal growth and change as enhancements rather than deviations from societal expectations.

Throughout the duration of the assignment, I learned to utilize various methods to both encourage and direct me towards a completed essay that was satisfactory to my standards. In my writing process, I incorporated both simple methods to clear my mind of distraction and active writing exercises to plan and begin the essay. After viewing and analyzing selections from the "This I Believe" essays, but before I began to plan and prewrite, I educated myself on the principles of MLA format and practiced implementation with blank documents, and mock sentences and paragraphs.

The prewriting and planning included exercises that applied various tones and themes to different beliefs that appeared to meet the requirements of the assignment. This prewriting exercise is a major process that I use in every writing I do, from poetry, fiction, and non-fiction. In these short exercises, I give myself prompts with a range of topics, and respond to them using different tones of writing. In a sense reflective to trying on different outfits, I practiced different tones by alternating between writing past and present tense, formal and informal, serious and humorous, and optimistic and pessimistic. For finding the style of writing I tested the use of descriptive versus expository writing. After these exercises I felt equipped to select a topic, tone, and style, and began to craft the rough draft, examine the given and received peer reviews, and finally create my polished drafts.

Project Two

A rhetorical analysis of an advertisement served as the basis for the second essay. The chosen advertisement had to meet the requirements of having been targeted towards an American audience and developed in the past twenty years. The focus was on employing a rhetorical framework to decipher the ad's features and assert a claim regarding its reflection of twenty-first-century American values. After using various keywords in search for a suitable advertisement, I found an ad by the gun control organization, Brady United. In my essay "Civilian Soldiers: Assault Weapons in Society" I analyzed the advertisement for an assault weapon ban. The single image was made remarkable by the prominent feature of the casket draped in an American Flag residing in a nightclub. The rhetorical analysis essay examined the symbolism, color choices, and rhetorical appeals used in the ad to convey a message and emotional impact of assault weapons on civilian life. It discusses how the ad connects to historical events, legal battles, and the current fight for stricter gun control laws.

Before I began planning and writing this essay, I first had to acquire an understanding that each detail of the advertisement had significant meaning. Realizing that in a major project such as Brady United's ad nothing was conveniently or ignorantly included, allowed for a deeper understanding of the company that produced or endorsed it. In the assault weapon ban advertisement, the included call-to-action words were not the only ways to decipher the intended meaning. By observing and investigating the many background features and elements, I was able to see a story greater than the two-dimensional photo. At the very least, the analysis required brief research into the company and the topic of the advertisement. The rough draft included the bare minimum of research, which resulted in me being extremely dissatisfied with the lack of substance included in the essay. To grow the analyses, I began to investigate Brady United's ideals and goals more thoroughly than before. Researching the company quickly became researching the founders and then almost every side and background story I could find.

After analyzing Brady United and their reasons for creating the advertisement, I began to research the history of American gun control laws, the history of assault weapons, and various statistics of the rates of gun violence and mass shootings. By researching as I did, the analysis was able to relate to the audience of the essay, as well as Brady's intended audience of the advertisement.

Project Three

The third essay was an exploratory essay with a delayed-thesis which grew to be one of my favorite writings as of yet. With this assignment, I attempted to reflect on my personal experiences and explore the concept of Growth and Fixed mindsets, particularly in the context of Dr. Carol Dweck's theory. By acknowledging the impact of failures on personal growth, I emphasized that success is often intertwined with setbacks. "Growth and Fixed Mindsets: Exploring Their Relation to Neurodiversity" delved into a discussion of whether Dweck's theory could be applied to a diverse range of individuals, particularly focusing on those with neurodiversites and disabilities. Before delving into the summaries and responses to the five sources, the assignment required an exploratory question to be clearly stated after the personal and reflective introduction. With the complexity of the subject explored, I felt it would be a better fit to ask two related questions rather than one.

After viewing the first of the required sources, "You Can Grow Your Brain" by Lisa Blackwell, I found the direction and tone I wanted the essay to acquire. Deciding to critically approach the Growth and Fixed mindset theory, I identified a problem, "…this seemingly simple reductionistic theory attempts to fill the massiveness of the thinking and workings of individual minds, without considering the many variations of the brain which can influence the mind." I questioned the "one-size-fits-all" mentality and suggested that the theory, while promoting a positive attitude, may inadvertently contribute to discrimination against those with limitations. The two questions I asked served as pathways for which the problem could be dismissed or solved. I first asked a base question, "With recognition of neurodiversity, can Dr. Dweck's theory apply to more than the conventional person?" The second question was an extension of the first, "If not, what are the possible ways to improve the inclusivity of the theory and incorporate those often overlooked?" Its purpose was to balance out the critical exploration by looking for solutions to alleviate the identified problem, if I found that it continued to persist throughout the duration of the explorations. After the exploration and reflections, the essay concluded with the delayed-thesis which served as a response to the two questions asked at the beginning.

Similar to the writing process I'd gone through during the drafting of "I Live For Me", the planning and prewriting for the exploratory essay began with finding and adjusting different writing tones and styles. One of the first prewriting tasks assigned was to draft the first two paragraphs, the personal introduction, of the essay as well as the exploratory questions. During this I initially attempted to introduce the essay in a philosophical tone. The prewriting paragraphs used language that prompted the audience to engage in self-reflection with a multitude of rhetorical questions that ranged from societal and cultural beliefs to philosophical questions. After finding that the initial paragraph drafts didn't fit into the identified problem, I began to review more of the assigned sources. As I'd decided to completely change the course of my essay, I had to find more suitable sources. Going through the sources I began to re-summarize them while attempting to view them in a different light. After doing this process, sometimes doing so multiple times, I was able to find the five sources I would use that would suit my critical analysis of Dweck's theory.

Learning Outcomes for Comp I

Throughout this semester, I've learned how and why to assess the environment of a writing and appropriately adapt it to be understandable to audiences other than myself. By understanding the intended or expected audience, I could tailor my reflections, analyses, and explorations to resonate with the perceptions and values of that specific demographic. After going through and rereading the rough and polished draft of the first essay assignment, I've noticed improvement in audience recognition and how it shaped the transformations of the language and extended explanations used. In the drafts of the first essay, "I Live For Me", this growth could be observed through the evolution of descriptive language and details.

In the rough draft I wrote:

"I'd gotten it into my head that it was up to me to avenge my parent's image and name from my own tarnish by doing away with everything that had gone wrong. This felt like an impossible task as 'everything that had gone wrong' seemed to be every piece of my being… They deserved better, and I continually chanted, 'Momma's sacrificed so much,' like a constant, war-cry that'd become the theme song of my days. I had to fix it; I couldn't live with myself knowing the burden I was on everyone."

The original paragraph didn't explain much to the audience about my parents and why I held the beliefs that I did. To recognize the inherent ignorance readers would have, especially when reflecting on personal topics, the polished draft expanded on this topic by creating a dedicated paragraph that gave context to my parents and my mindset.

Critical thinking, reading, and composing were foundational to my progress in this course. During the planning and writing of the second and third essays, "Civilian Soldiers: Assault Weapons in Society" and "Growth and Fixed Mindsets: Exploring Their Relation to Neurodiversity", I was able to use supporting readings and sources to form an understanding of the assigned topics. For both essays I delved into research, synthesizing information from multiple sources to develop an informed opinion. This process was a testament to the seamless integration of reading, inquiry, and communication in the creation of a well-informed piece. In "Growth and Fixed Mindsets: Exploring Their Relation to Neurodiversity", I utilized this skill to show fluctuations in opinions and progress towards answering my exploratory questions.

In an example of using critical thinking and analysis to merge the topic of Nicole Lynn Lewis' "How Colleges Tell Student-Parents They Don't Belong" with my own. While Lewis' article focused on the disadvantages student-parents are presented with in colleges and universities, I was able to find phrases and moments that could also be applied to my topic of neurodiversity.

To show this, I wrote:

"While this research and recognition of the struggles of student-parents is an important discussion, I also saw how relatable the mentioned struggles and hardships are to those that do not fit into the confines of yet another mold… In another parallel I found between Lewis' depiction and the exclusion of the neurodiverse, Lewis described the ways of indirect oppression. Lewis said, "Sometimes the hurdles were subtle… Other times, the hurdles were so significant that they threatened my ability to stay enrolled." Her descriptions of the challenges that come with someone attempting to survive in an environment that is not equipped for them, are easily recognizable."

Another thing the second and third essays underscored was my ability to integrate original ideas with those of others. An example could be seen in the third essay where I engaged with diverse sources to weave together information and additional readings about or relating to Dr. Carol Dweck's Growth and Fixed mindsets theory. Using MLA guidance, I've improved in my implementation of appropriately accrediting the who, what, when, and where of ideas that weren't my own.

Before beginning this course, I tended to shy away from rough drafts and planning, instead opting for a one-time writing approach. However, recognizing the value of iteration and refinement, I embraced the practice of preplanning and then generating multiple drafts. Unlike high school, the prewriting techniques taught in Composition I weren't requirements but instead suggestions to guide the development of personal writing practices. Understanding this made me realize that planning and prewriting to some extent, even just a paragraph, can go a long way to make the draft writing process smoother.

Mentioned as a writing process for the first essay, trying on different themes, tones, and styles is a major generation technique that I've gained. The integration of this method resulted in a more prewriting than I'd previously thought I could tolerate and prevented my common practice of completely restarting a writing piece after finding I no longer agreed with the tone or style.

The system of peer reviewing was an effective method of reflection as it resulted in me gaining further insight and understanding of assignment objectives. Reviewing the essays of classmates required a transition into being a reader rather than the writer, which allowed me to recognize what I was missing, doing incorrectly, or didn't like about my own essay. Receiving my reviewed essay had similar effects, though the most notable advantage was criticism of sentences, transitions, and paragraphs that were unclear. Understanding these faults resulted in me being able to further connect with the intended audience. The emphasis on collaborative writing processes in this course significantly influenced my approach to feedback and revision.

A fluent essay is first formed on foundations that consist of a variety of writing pieces that form the overall "feel." Incorporating revision of grammar, punctuation, and spelling, with bigger concerns such as deviations of tones, allows for a comprehensive essay. When revising my essays, I first focus on the bigger concerns, as they usually affect progression and readability. After assuring the essay transitions and continues in an appropriate manner, I then assess the essays for small mistakes that have a lower chance of interrupting audience attention, such as spelling, grammar, and formatting. Through these varied writing tasks, I have cultivated a heightened awareness of conventions, contributing to the overall polish and coherence of my compositions.

Strengths and Challenges

The strengths that I could identify in the polished drafts of all three projects are indicated as having exhibited significant improvement from rough drafts and past writings. For the first essay, I was able to showcase enhanced clarity and flow by delving into personal experiences with refined language and emphasizing the theme of self-acceptance. The second essay, the rhetorical analysis, effectively engaged the audience through vivid descriptions, introspective symbolism analysis, and a compelling investigation of the ads call to action. Essay three displayed an improvement in organization and refined language which contributed to an increasingly professional and engaging presentation. All the assignments aimed to display my ability to reflect on feedback, leading to critical analysis and deeper insights into the respective topics, and I believe that was achieved to satisfactory standards.

Some common challenges I had with all the projects included having a significantly hard time effectively transmitting my ideas and themes in a concise manner. One thing I had specifically challenged myself with this semester was condensing my writing and language and attempting to adhere to the specified word counts—or at least not overly exceeding them. Another challenge was my use of tone consistency throughout the writings. I tend to "write as I talk", meaning my writing reflects the style in which I speak. When speaking, my voice flits between volume, infliction, and variation in tone. I realize that this style of speaking can sometimes make my intentions and meanings difficult to decipher to those I am speaking to. Similarly, this style of writing impairs my ability to create smooth transitions between sentences, ideas, and paragraphs. Specific to the second and third essays, improvement could be made by further balancing of the evaluation of theories, introducing in-text citations, and providing concise explanations where needed as these would enhance the overall quality and seamless readability.

Overall Benefit

Throughout the course, I've observed a remarkable growth in my writing skills and critical thinking abilities, evident across the three distinct projects. Project 1, the "This I Believe" essay, challenged me to construct a narrative with precision, emphasizing personal beliefs in a format and description an unassociated audience could clearly follow and understand. In Project 2, the Rhetorical Analysis essay, I honed my skills to perform a meticulous examination and illustration of an advertisement from Brady United. Crafting this essay required a comprehensive exploration of the rhetorical features and an assertion regarding their reflection of twenty-first-century American values. The project strengthened my ability to convey complex ideas effectively. Project 3, the Exploratory Essay, marked a notable shift toward research-driven exploration and delayed-thesis composition. This project encouraged a deep dive into current research on failure, growth mindset, and their implications. The varied genres and rhetorical situations covered in the course broadened my understanding of effective communication, fostering adaptability in tailoring my writing to diverse audiences.
